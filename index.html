Executive brief
The story for TEGNA

A client-ready narrative covering the key insights from April — written for the QBR conversation.

Campaign intelligence brief · Spectrum Retirement Communities · April 2026 · TEGNA / WBNS

Four communities driving 65% of conversions — and why that's the right story

April's portfolio performance is anchored by four communities that together generated 1,584 of 2,223 total conversions: Dougherty Ferry (430), Ocotillo (404), Creve Coeur (378), and Park Meadows/Aventura (372). These numbers look exceptional — and they are — but they require context to tell the right story to the client.

Dougherty Ferry and Creve Coeur's totals are substantially driven by Home Page click-through conversions: 304 click-throughs for Dougherty Ferry and 270 for Creve Coeur. These reflect strong audience engagement with the homepage pixel but are distinct in character from deeper funnel signals like floorplan views or pricing page visits. When TEGNA asks "why did these communities perform so well?", the honest answer is: their homepage audience is highly engaged and clicking through at exceptional rates — which is a genuine sign of intent, but not the same signal as someone who explored a floorplan or compared pricing.

Ocotillo tells a different story: 402 of its 404 conversions came through SiteRT — people who had already visited the website and were served a retargeted ad. At 116 conversions per 1,000 SiteRT impressions, it is the most efficient retargeting campaign in the portfolio.

SiteRT is the portfolio's most powerful — and most underused — tool

The retargeting data reveals a structural opportunity. SiteRT and Site RT ad groups combined delivered 874 conversions from approximately 29,600 impressions — a rate of 29.5 conversions per 1,000 impressions. Every other ad group type combined: 1,349 conversions from 5.07 million impressions — a rate of 0.27 per 1,000. SiteRT is 109 times more efficient per impression than cold audience targeting.

When SiteRT conversion counts decline month-over-month, the reason is almost always one of three things: site traffic dropped (fewer people in the retargeting pool), SiteRT impression volume was reduced, or the retargeting window expired before conversion. This framework should guide any TEGNA conversation about SiteRT fluctuations.

View-through attribution — where floorplan intent lives

Of the 437 floorplan conversion events recorded in April, 341 (78%) were view-through. This is the most important attribution insight in the dataset. Families exploring floorplan options for a senior living community are doing so after seeing an ad — not because they clicked one. The ad created awareness and intent; the visit happened independently. This is why view-through attribution matters for this category and why it should never be stripped from TEGNA's reporting as "less valuable" than click-through.

Home page conversions follow the opposite pattern: 66% click-through. This reflects the direct-response nature of homepage visits — the ad prompted an immediate action. Neither pattern is better; they describe different moments in a complex, long-duration purchase decision that often involves multiple family members.

Geographic standouts — Kansas punches above its weight

Missouri led all states with 841 conversions across 6 communities, reflecting both the volume of Dougherty Ferry and Creve Coeur and the strength of behavioral targeting in established suburban St. Louis markets. Arizona followed with 521 conversions across 8 communities. But the geographic efficiency story belongs to Kansas: with only 255k impressions — the second-smallest state budget — Shawnee Hills and Park Meadows together generated 382 conversions and achieved a ZIP-level CTR of 4.15% in 66214 (Shawnee), the highest single-ZIP engagement rate in the entire national portfolio.

Footfall: 658 physical visits, and the Palos Heights story

Foot traffic attribution confirmed 658 physical property visits across 26 communities. Gilbert (AZ) led with 41 visits — a story completely invisible from conversion data alone. Lincoln Meadows recorded a 70-minute average dwell time among its 4 footfall visitors; Dougherty Ferry averaged 61 minutes. These are not accidental walk-ins. When TEGNA asks about communities with seemingly low digital conversion counts, the footfall data is often the missing context — Palos Heights, for example, generated only 6 digital conversions but 33 unique physical visitors, suggesting an audience that converts in person rather than online.

Forward-looking actions
Three conversations to have with TEGNA
1
Standardise SiteRT naming conventions
'SiteRT' and 'Site RT' appear as separate ad groups across the 38 advertisers, fragmenting what should be a single retargeting story. Aligning naming conventions would make the 109× efficiency advantage immediately visible in any reporting view without manual aggregation.
2
Present footfall as a primary KPI for high-dwell communities
For communities like Palos Heights, Lincoln Meadows, and Gilbert — where footfall visits are disproportionate to digital conversions — the narrative should lead with the physical visit story. These communities should be evaluated on footfall + dwell time, not conversion count alone.
3
Add context to Dougherty Ferry & Creve Coeur home page numbers
The 304 and 270 click-through home page conversions are real and valuable, but proactively contextualising them prevents the client from treating these totals as equivalent to deeper-funnel signals. A brief note in the dashboard or report preserves trust and prevents "why did it drop?" questions next month.
